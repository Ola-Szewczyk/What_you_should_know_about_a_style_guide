The elements of a style guide

The author has to now choose which elements are making it into the document. Except for the introduction, the order of the elements in a style guide may vary.

Introduction

It’s part of the document that is especially useful in organizations with big documentation teams. It explains the reasons behind the document—who created it and what for. New employees to the team may find it very useful, as well as other audiences, for example, clients.

How-to section

The aim here is to instruct the users on how to effectively use it. Everything they should know about the style guide should be mentioned in this section.

History of updates

As mentioned before, a style guide should be modified on a regular basis. Each change should be mentioned in this part of the document. A simple table is usually enough to keep a record of the changes.

Voice

This extremely important section of a style guide focuses on how the organization talks to its customers through its documents. The team in charge of creating a style guide should take some time to carefully define the voice that will convince the reader to read the whole message and feel it was created by regular people rather than robots.

Terminology and word lists

Especially in big organizations as well as in companies operating within specified areas (for example, green power or medicine) it’s crucial to gather the most commonly used vocabulary and phrases so that every writer can refer to it and use it in the same way across different documents. This section may also include an explanation of how the numbers and symbols are used.

Accessibility

From this part of a style guide, the writer will learn how to create deliverables that are accessible, diverse, and inclusive. The set of rules included in this section should be detailed enough to enable writers to create documents that don’t discriminate and improve accessibility. This section may even contain a set of better-choice words and phrases.

There may be many more elements that will create a style guide. Style guides available online cover topics such as grammar, formatting principles, and even jargon. The awareness and good preparation could save a lot of time and help to avoid including unnecessary elements that aren’t relevant from the point of view of the organization.
